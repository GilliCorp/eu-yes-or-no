## BOTTOM LINE

Fisheries is the area where membership changes the **most**, and it's the reason the 2010–2013 talks effectively died. Today fisheries management is an **exclusively national** matter; as a member it becomes an **EU exclusive competence** under the **Common Fisheries Policy (CFP, Regulation (EU) No 1380/2013)** — the setting of **Total Allowable Catches (TACs)**, technical measures, and the **allocation of quotas** would be decided at **Union level**, not in Reykjavík.

The distributional rule is **"relative stability":** each year's TAC is split into national quota shares as **fixed percentages anchored to historic catch records** (the 1983 key, reference years 1973–78). So Iceland's shares would track its **track record**, not be freely negotiated. Outside a **12-nautical-mile** coastal band the default is **equal access** for all EU vessels — the heart of the "control over our waters" fear — though *actual catch* is still bounded by quota shares, not open competition (an important nuance: equal access ≠ unlimited foreign catch).

On special terms, the precedent is **unfavourable to a permanent carve-out:** no accession candidate has ever won a permanent derogation from a common policy; only temporary derogations (changeable at Union level) exist, and any permanent "special solution" must be **written into the accession treaty** (Malta's 25-nm zone is the small-state precedent — but it's a *regulated-access* measure for small vessels, amendable at Union level, **not** a flag-based exclusion).

Fisheries carries **outsized weight:** ~**39–40% of goods exports** while employing only ~**7%** of the workforce. In the 2010–2013 talks the **fisheries chapter was never even opened.**

**The core both-sides tension:** the post-2013 CFP is more decentralised and (per the IES report) arguably *compatible* with Iceland's quota system **versus** loss of autonomy over setting TACs, decided at Union level where Iceland's weight would be very small — with no permanent derogation ever granted.

## TODAY (already true) vs AS A MEMBER

### Today — fisheries is national
- ✅ Fisheries management in Icelandic waters is an **exclusively national** matter. Iceland runs its own quota system, sets its own TACs, and advocates **independently** in international bodies (e.g. NEAFC, the mackerel disputes). *(Foreign Ministry Summary Conclusions)*

### As a member — under the CFP
- ✅ The CFP is an **EU exclusive competence** (TFEU Art. 3(1)(d)): setting and allocating TACs/quotas plus conservation and technical measures are decided **at Union level**. Legal basis **Reg. (EU) 1380/2013**, in force since 1 Jan 2014. Iceland's own MFA states "the European Union alone holds the competence to conserve resources," read broadly to cover maximum catches, technical measures, and quota allocation among Member States. TAC fixing stays the **Council's** annual competence (Art. 43(3) TFEU). *(EUR-Lex CELEX:32013R1380; EP factsheet 114; Summary Conclusions)*
- ✅ **Relative stability** (Art. 16(1)): each Member State gets a **fixed-percentage share** of each stock's TAC, "stable over time," locked to **historic catch records** — the 1983 allocation key (ref. catches 1973–78, grounded in the 1976 Council Resolution), **left unchanged by the 2013 reform**. Practical effect: Iceland's shares would track its track record, not be freely negotiated. *(EUR-Lex; EP factsheet 114; ICES Journal; ScienceDirect)*
- ✅ **Equal access** outside 12 nm (Art. 5(1)): "Union fishing vessels shall have equal access to waters and resources in all Union waters." Within **12 nm**, states may restrict fishing to vessels traditionally fishing from adjacent ports — a derogation **extended to 31 Dec 2032** by Reg. (EU) 2022/2495. **Nuance:** equal access governs *access to waters*; catch is still bound by quota shares. *(EUR-Lex; EP factsheet 114)*

## THE LEDGER — gains, losses, uncertain

### Gains / opportunities (good-faith pro-EU side)
- ✅ **The post-2013 CFP is more decentralised**, and per the Icelandic **Institute of Economic Studies (IES)** report, the reformed EU fisheries management system is **compatible with Iceland's** quota-based system. *(EP briefing EXPO-AFET_SP(2014)522331)*
- ✅ **Quota shares are predictable**, not a free-for-all: relative stability gives each state a stable percentage; equal access does **not** mean unlimited foreign catch. *(EUR-Lex; EP factsheet 114)*

### Losses / risks (good-faith anti-EU side)
- ✅ **Loss of autonomy over setting TACs.** The core Icelandic objection: TACs would be decided **at Union level**, where Iceland's **negotiating weight would be very small**. *(EP briefing; Summary Conclusions)*
- ✅ **No permanent derogation has ever been granted** from a common policy to any accession candidate — only temporary ones (changeable at Union level); permanence requires explicit treaty anchoring. *(Summary Conclusions; cf. Malta)*
- ✅ **The Malta precedent is limited:** the 25-nm management zone restricts access to small (<12 m) non-towed-gear vessels (capped ~1,950 vessels / 83,000 kW / 4,035 GT on a 2000–01 baseline), set via the Act of Accession and **amendable at Union level** — a geographic/vessel-characteristic regulated-access regime, **not** a permanent flag-based exclusion. *(Council Reg. (EC) 813/2004)*
- ✅ **Independent international advocacy at stake.** The MFA flagged "formal authority over marine resources, limitations to investments and advocacy at international level" as the hard issues. *(Summary Conclusions)*

### The economic stakes (why this is THE decisive issue)
- ✅ Fisheries = ~**39–40% of goods exports** (39.3% in 2011, 40% in 2012) while employing only ~**7%** of the workforce (2012); primary sector (agriculture+fisheries+mining) = **6.8% of GDP**. *(EP briefing)* **Caveat:** the 39–40% is share of **merchandise/goods** exports — including services the share is lower (~17% in 2017).

### The 2010–2013 record (a blank, not a verdict)
- ✅ The fisheries chapter — "the most sensitive of all" — was **never opened.** At suspension (22 May 2013) the Commission had not submitted its screening/review report, so Iceland **never tabled a formal position** on fisheries. The MFA judged an agreement "would have been difficult." *(Summary Conclusions; EP briefing)*

## IMPORTANT CAVEATS (carry into the chapter copy)

1. **State the 2032 date.** The 12-nm coastal-access derogation read as expiring 31 Dec 2022 in the original 1380/2013 text but was **extended to 31 Dec 2032** by Reg. (EU) 2022/2495 — say this so readers don't think coastal protection lapsed. *(A claim framing it as a lapsed/time-limited right was **refuted 0-3** — see below.)*
2. **Equal access ≠ unlimited foreign catch.** Don't let readers infer open competition; catch is bound by relative-stability quota shares.
3. **The 39–40% figure is GOODS exports**, not total exports including services (~17% in 2017). Label it precisely.
4. **Malta is a regulated-access zone, not an exclusion.** A strong-form "non-discriminatory by nationality" framing was **refuted 0-3**; present it as geographic/vessel-characteristic regulated access obtained at accession.
5. **Relative-stability shares are "fixed percentages" as a layperson gloss**, but Art. 16(3) permits adjustments for significant disparities, and the 1983 key also reflected jurisdictional-loss compensation and Hague Preferences, not pure catch history.
6. **The autonomy/small-weight concern is the *general Icelandic objection*** — attributing it verbatim to the IES report was **refuted 1-2** (overstated). Present it as the general objection, and the IES "compatible system" point as the pro side.
7. **Facts frozen at 2013 suspension;** application later withdrawn. CFP remains 1380/2013 (amended, not repealed) as of 2026.
