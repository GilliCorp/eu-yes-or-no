## BOTTOM LINE

Accession is **mostly "take it as offered."** Joining the EU means adopting the **entire existing body of EU law** (the *acquis*), split into **35 negotiating chapters**, in full. A candidate does **not** negotiate *whether* the rules apply — only **how and when** it implements them (timelines, transitional periods, technical adaptations). The acquis itself is not on the table.

**Genuine lasting flexibility is real but narrow.** A time-limited **transitional period** is comparatively easy to get — but it **expires**. A **permanent carve-out** has to be written into the **Accession Treaty / Act of Accession** as a derogation or protocol, and that requires the **unanimous agreement of every existing member state, the European Parliament's consent, and ratification by all members** — a very high bar. Most "special deals" invoked in public debate were **temporary transitions that have since expired**, not permanent wins.

**The "peek in the package" claim is half-true.** Opening negotiations does **not** legally force a country to join — a candidate can walk away, and **Iceland did exactly that** (opened talks June 2011, suspended May 2013, withdrew 2015). So "just looking" is literally true. **But** the structure favours the EU heavily, especially for a small, trade-dependent state, and — in **Iceland's own government's words** — there was *"no obvious reason to assume that a derogation from these rules could be gained."* And tellingly, in three years of talks Iceland's single most vital chapter — **fisheries — was never even opened.**

**Permanent carve-outs do exist** (so the anti-EU "literally never any wiggle room" line is too strong): **Malta's second-home Protocol** and **Denmark's opt-outs** are real and permanent. But they are **rare, narrow, and anchored in treaty protocols** rather than won in the ordinary chapter-by-chapter bargaining — and Denmark's famous opt-outs were **not** accession terms at all (they came later, at Maastricht 1992).

**The honest verdict is a map, not a victory for either side:** the *acquis* is essentially non-negotiable (anti-EU correct on the big picture); permanent carve-outs are obtainable but rare and hard (pro-EU has real precedent, but not an easy one).

## THE CORE DISPUTE, ADJUDICATED

**Pro-EU claim:** "We can negotiate a good deal with tailored carve-outs."
**Anti-EU claim:** "There's practically no wiggle room — you take the acquis as offered."

**Verdict on the evidence:** The anti-EU side is closer to right **on the big picture** — the acquis is adopted in full and is not itself negotiable; negotiation is about timing and implementation. But the anti-EU claim in its **strongest form is false**: permanent derogations *have* been granted (Malta, Denmark), so it is wrong to say they are impossible. The pro-EU side is right that **carve-outs are achievable**, but wrong if it implies they are **easy or broad** — they are rare, narrow, treaty-anchored, and require unanimity + EP consent + ratification by all. Both bumper stickers fail; the sourced middle is what this chapter delivers.

## TODAY (the mechanics) — what is fixed vs what flexes

### Fixed by design (non-negotiable)
- ✅ **The acquis is adopted in full.** The Commission: "The acquis is divided into 35 chapters for accession negotiations"; accession is the "adoption of established EU law, preparations to be in a position to properly apply and enforce it." The candidate accepts it in full and negotiates only **how and when** to implement. *(EC enlargement: chapters-acquis; steps-towards-joining; glossary)* **Nuance:** "every domain of national policy" overstates — direct taxation, healthcare, education, culture and sport remain largely national competence.
- ✅ **The acquis applies by default from the date of accession.** 2003 Act of Accession, Art. 2: the acquis is "binding on the new Member States from the date of accession"; Art. 10: its application is subject only to the **derogations expressly provided for in the Act**. The Act structurally separates **permanent provisions** from **temporary/transitional provisions (Part Four, Arts 24–42)**. *(EUR-Lex CELEX:12003T/TXT)* **Nuance:** Part Three covers adaptations to *secondary acts*, not "to the Treaties."
- ✅ **Every chapter must close, and closure needs unanimity.** "No negotiations on any individual chapter are closed until every EU government is satisfied." Opening and closing are Council acts each member state can veto. *(EC: steps-towards-joining)*
- ✅ **Benchmarks gate each chapter.** The Commission sets **opening benchmarks** (conditions before talks in a field can start) and **closing benchmarks** (conditions before a chapter can close). Same framework used for the 15 June 2026 EU-Ukraine cluster opening. *(EC: steps-towards-joining)*
- ✅ **It's conditionality, not a bargain between equals.** Canonical enlargement scholarship (Schimmelfennig & Sedelmeier, *External Incentives Model*): "The EU sets the adoption of its norms and rules as conditions ... The EU is free to choose both the conditions and the reward ... The target states are free to accept or reject." Bargaining power flows from **asymmetric interdependence**, working strongly in the EU's favour — most acutely for small, trade-dependent candidates. *(EUI/Schimmelfennig-Sedelmeier)* **Nuance:** a theoretical idealization — in practice candidates *do* bargain over how/when.

### Where flexibility actually lives
- ✅ **Permanent carve-outs ARE a recognised possible outcome — but hard.** Turkey's 2005 Negotiating Framework: "Long transitional periods, derogations, specific arrangements or permanent safeguard clauses ... may be considered," while "these negotiations are an open-ended process, the outcome of which cannot be guaranteed beforehand." A permanent carve-out must be a protocol/derogation in the Accession Treaty → **unanimity + EP consent + ratification by every member**. *(Council st20002/05; EUR-Lex 12003T/TXT)*
- ✅ **Temporary vs permanent is THE distinction.** A transition states "for a period of X years" and then expires; a permanent derogation has no sunset and is treaty-anchored. Most "special deals" in public rhetoric were the expiring kind.

## CONCRETE PRECEDENTS (verified this pass)

### Permanent, treaty-anchored carve-outs — real but narrow
- ✅ **Malta — second homes.** **Protocol No 6** to the 2003 Act of Accession (CELEX:12003T/PRO/06, OJ L 236, 23 Sep 2003, "In force"): "Malta may on a non-discriminatory basis maintain in force the rules on the acquisition and holding of immovable property for secondary residence purposes." **No sunset clause** — a genuine **permanent** carve-out. Only the monetary price threshold is revisable, not the derogation itself. *(EUR-Lex CELEX:12003T/PRO/06)*
- ✅ **Denmark — euro/EMU opt-out.** A **permanent treaty-protocol opt-out** — but negotiated at **Maastricht (1992)**, after the 2 Jun 1992 referendum (50.7% no) and the Edinburgh Agreement, **NOT** a term of Denmark's 1973 accession. All four Danish opt-outs (EMU, defence/CSDP, JHA, citizenship) arose post-Maastricht. Protocol (No 16): the abrogation procedure "shall only be initiated at the request of Denmark." *(EUR-Lex: denmark-emu-opt-out; CELEX:12016M/PRO/16)* **This is the key teaching point: ACCESSION-era terms vs LATER treaty opt-outs are different things — Denmark's opt-outs are often miscited as accession deals.**

### Iceland's own 2010–2013 record (the empirical test of "just peeking")
- ✅ **You can open talks and walk away.** Negotiations opened **June 2011**, halted by the new government **22 May 2013**, application withdrawn 2015. At suspension: **27 chapters opened, 11 provisionally closed**, 6 not opened; no negotiating position was in place for **agriculture, fisheries, free movement of capital, and right of establishment**. *(Iceland MFA Summary Conclusions; EP briefing EXPO-AFET_SP(2014)522331)* **Caveat:** chapter arithmetic varies by source (27 + 6 = 33; the full acquis has 35; chapters 34/35 are administrative and often excluded). Present figures as quoted, not as summing to 35.
- ✅ **The most important chapter was never reached.** **Fisheries (Ch. 13) was never opened** — the Commission had not submitted its screening report, so Iceland never tabled a position — leaving its most vital sector (~39% of goods exports) entirely unnegotiated when talks stopped. *(MFA Summary Conclusions; EP briefing; LoC Global Legal Monitor)*
- ✅ **Iceland's government's own words** are the strongest rebuttal to "an easy tailored deal": accession "would follow the formal rules currently in force. There was **no obvious reason to assume that a derogation from these rules could be gained** or the formal process accelerated ... These are therefore **not altogether traditional negotiations**." *(MFA Summary Conclusions)*
