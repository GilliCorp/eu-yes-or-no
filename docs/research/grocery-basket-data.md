## Tariff / protection figures (the EU-entry lever)
- Average agricultural tariff **~19.7–23.5%** vs **0.1%** non-agricultural.
- **Dairy ~73%**, **meat/live animals ~62–67%**, **eggs ~30%** (highest *proportional* support — 62% of egg gross farm receipts), **potatoes ~30% + up to ISK 1,087/kg**, **tomatoes/cucumbers/peppers seasonally** high to protect geothermal greenhouses.
- Compound duties common: 30% ad valorem + ISK 5–1,462/kg specific.
- **Best illustrative basis for "how much could protected prices fall":** OECD Producer Nominal Protection Coefficient **1.60** — Icelandic farm-gate prices are ~**60% above** world/border reference. So in the *limit* of full exposure, protected-good prices could be up to ~**38% lower** — but realistically far less, after **pass-through** (importers/retailers keep margin), transport, retail costs, and domestic production continuing. Use a **wide, modest, clearly-illustrative range**, never the raw tariff %.
- Sources: WTO Tariff Profile (Iceland) 2024; OECD *Agricultural Policy Monitoring & Evaluation 2025 — Iceland*; USDA FAS *Review of Agricultural Tariffs — Iceland* (2017); accession Chapter 11; Lloyds Bank Trade (seasonal veg); WTO TPR 2017 (87 TRQs).

## Median income anchor (for the affordability lens)
- **826,000 ISK/month** — median full-time regular wages, 2025 (Hagstofa, "Dreifing launa á íslenskum vinnumarkaði 2025", pub. 13 May 2026). Mean was 913,000; 64% earn below the mean.
- Earlier: 753–758,000 ISK/month (full-time, 2024); median annual personal income ≈ 692,000 ISK/month (all individuals, 2024).
- ⚠️ **Median household *disposable* income is not published** as a headline figure (data gap). Use the median full-time wage as the anchor and **label it precisely** ("miðgildi reglulegra heildarlauna í fullu starfi") — don't call it "household income".
- Sources: Hagstofa / statice.is wage + income releases (2025–2026).

## Biosecurity / animal-health rules (the strong both-sides angle)
**Restrictions (historic → current):** prior authorisation + 30-day −18°C freeze on raw/fresh meat; Salmonella-negative certification for raw eggs; unpasteurised dairy banned. After court rulings, the blanket ban/authorisation was lifted (Reg. 1250/2019) **but Iceland kept ESA-approved Salmonella guarantees** (Jan 2019) for poultry meat, turkey, eggs — same as Finland/Sweden/Norway.

**EFTA chronology (a real "direction of travel", not hypothetical):** ESA inquiry 2011 → formal notice Oct 2013 (breach of Dir. 89/662/EEC) → **EFTA Court E-17/15** (1 Feb 2016: raw-meat import-authorisation breaches EEA law) → damages to importer (Nov 2016) → ESA infringement **E-2/17 & E-3/17** (14 Nov 2017: meat, eggs AND dairy rules must change — first successful ESA infringement vs Iceland) → 2019 reform.

**View A (pro-liberalisation):** blanket barriers went beyond food-safety need (products already cleared EU inspection still frozen 30 days); ESA found no scientific justification; protection inflates prices (OECD PSE >3× OECD average); the farming lobby (Bændasamtök) pushed to keep them while importers/consumers paid.

**View B (pro-protection):** Iceland's animal population is genuinely disease-naive — free of 14 WOAH-listed diseases (FMD, ASF, CSF, HPAI, Newcastle…); livestock meet ~1/5 the infectious agents of continental herds; **lowest veterinary antibiotic use in Europe** (Spain ~80× more); only 21 domestic Salmonella cases in 2017; doctors/scientists warned raw-meat imports endanger this. Stronger-than-average import scrutiny is an evidence-based biosecurity interest.

**Sources:** MAST import rules; ESA press (Jan 2019 Salmonella guarantees; 2013 notice); EFTA Court E-17/15 and joined E-2/17 & E-3/17; CFIA disease-freedom (Mar 2025); FoodNavigator (2019 expert warnings); OECD 2025.
