## BOTTOM LINE

On natural-resource ownership, **EU membership changes far less than the "selling off the national wealth" fear implies — because the EEA already did most of it.** Free movement of capital and the right of establishment are **core EEA freedoms Iceland already lives under**, so EEA/EFTA nationals can *already* acquire Icelandic land, energy-utilisation rights, and salmon rivers largely on the same footing as Icelanders. Full membership adds **very little** on top of this specific axis.

**Public ownership is safe; non-discrimination is the real constraint.** **Article 345 TFEU** (mirrored by **Art. 125 EEA**, which already binds Iceland) means the EU is **neutral on public vs private ownership** — Iceland could keep Landsvirkjun fully public, and the EU "does not preclude" nationalisation or privatisation. **But** Art. 345 does **not** exempt national property rules from the treaty freedoms: per the **Essent ruling (C-105/12)**, a public-ownership regime still has to comply with **free movement of capital** and is judged on **non-discrimination + proportionality**. So Iceland's safeguards survive *if* they are non-discriminatory and proportionate — and that test **already applies under the EEA**, membership or not.

**On "who profits," Iceland keeps the keys.** **Taxation is a national competence requiring unanimity** at EU level — so the ability to design **resource fees / royalties (veiðigjald, auðlindagjald)** stays with Iceland; as a member it would even hold a **veto** on EU tax harmonisation. The constitutional **auðlindaákvæði** (2020 draft) declaring resources the property of the nation and mandating exploitation fees is **about ownership form and rent-capture, which Art. 345 protects** — its friction with EU law would be over *non-discriminatory access*, not over *public ownership* as such.

**The honest verdict:** the biggest ownership shifts people fear **already happened when Iceland joined the EEA**; full membership's *marginal* effect on resource ownership and resource rent is **small**. The genuinely unresolved knot is **fisheries-as-asset** — foreign-investment limits in the fishing sector were never negotiated and were expected to be **linked to the (never-opened) fisheries chapter** (see the fisheries + accession dossiers).

## THE CORE DISPUTE, ADJUDICATED

**Anti-EU:** "Membership means foreigners can buy up our land, rivers and energy — we hand over the national wealth."
**Pro-EU:** "Capital freedom is already EEA law; membership changes little, and Art. 345 lets us keep resources publicly owned."

**Verdict:** The pro-EU framing is closer to right **on this specific axis** — the capital/establishment freedoms that allow foreign acquisition are **already in force via the EEA**, so membership adds little. The anti-EU fear is **misdirected at "membership"** when the relevant change happened in 1994 with the EEA. **But** the anti-EU side has a real residual point: Iceland's domestic safeguards must stay **non-discriminatory and proportionate** to survive (they can't single out "foreigners"), and the **fisheries-as-asset** question (foreign ownership of quota) was never settled. Neither slogan survives intact; the truth is "mostly already true, with a real but narrow live question around fish."

## TODAY (already true via EEA) vs AS A MEMBER

### Today — the EEA already governs this
- ✅ **EEA/EFTA nationals can already acquire Icelandic real property WITHOUT ministerial permission** when exercising a treaty freedom (free movement of people, establishment, services, or capital) — they just file a declaration under Reg. No. 702/2002. *(government.is — foreign nationals' real-property rights)*
- ✅ **Energy-resource rights already open to the EEA:** under Iceland's 1991 Act on foreign investment, **hydropower and geothermal utilisation rights** may be held by Icelandic citizens/legal persons **OR persons resident in the EEA** — EEA nationals are already on the same footing as Icelanders. *(US State Dept 2025 Investment Climate — Iceland)*
- ✅ **Iceland's foreign-investment notification rules explicitly do NOT apply to EEA citizens/residents** — confirming EEA actors are already treated more favourably than other foreigners. *(US State Dept 2025)*
- ✅ **The capital/non-discrimination disciplines are identical under the EEA and EU.** The Commission's own case-law guide: as between EEA states, the rules "prohibiting restrictions on the movement of capital and discrimination… are identical to those under Community law," so national land-acquisition measures are "**no more exempt**… than under Community law." → **The same EU-style discipline already binds Iceland.** *(EC guide to free-movement-of-capital case law)*
- ✅ **Non-EEA foreigners still need permission** (e.g. UK citizens since Brexit, 31 Dec 2020) — showing the line that matters is **EEA vs non-EEA**, which membership doesn't move. *(government.is)*

### As a member — what actually changes
- Very little **on capital/establishment/ownership specifically** — these are already EEA freedoms. The material ownership change for resources happened at EEA accession, not at EU membership.
- The genuinely open item is **fisheries-as-asset** (foreign investment in the fishing sector) — never negotiated; see ❌/⚠️ and the fisheries dossier.

## THE LEDGER

### Reassurances / what's protected (good-faith pro-EU side)
- ✅ **Public ownership is safe — EU is neutral on it.** "The Treaties do not preclude, as a general rule, either the nationalisation of undertakings… or their privatisation." Iceland could keep Landsvirkjun public. *(EC case-law guide; Essent C-105/12)*
- ✅ **Art. 345 TFEU** (= **Art. 125 EEA**, already binding Iceland) expresses treaty **neutrality on the system of property ownership.** *(EC case-law guide)*
- ✅ **Resource rent stays Iceland's call.** Taxation is **largely national competence** — "the power to introduce, remove or adjust taxes remains in the hands of the Member States"; EU tax legislation needs **Council unanimity** → as a member Iceland would even hold a **veto**. *(EP factsheet 92 — general tax policy)*
- ✅ **Iceland already captures resource rent and it's constitutionally framed:** the **veiðigjald** is charged per kg of live-weight catch to "ensure the nation a share in the profit from utilization of the marine resources" *(island.is)*; in 2014 it was €52m = **6.0% of catch value / ~1.2% of Treasury revenue** *(ScienceDirect)*; ITQs since 1990 drove "public demand for the sharing of its resource rent" → fee since 2004 *(ScienceDirect)*.
- ✅ **The auðlindaákvæði** (2020 draft, Art. 1) declares resources "the property of the nation," bars acquiring them "for private ownership or permanent use," and mandates **fees for commercial-exploitation authorisations** — a constitutional basis for resource rent that **Art. 345 protects** as an ownership-system choice. *(Venice Commission CDL-REF(2020)049rev)*

### Constraints / risks (good-faith anti-EU side)
- ✅ **Art. 345 has a hard limit:** it "does not mean that rules governing the system of property ownership… are not subject to the fundamental rules of the FEU Treaty," incl. **non-discrimination, establishment, and free movement of capital** — a public-ownership regime is "**not free to disregard**… the rules relating to the free movement of capital." So safeguards must be **non-discriminatory + proportionate** to survive (Essent C-105/12). *(EC case-law guide)* — **but this already applies via the EEA.**
- ✅ **Fisheries ownership is the live exception:** Iceland limits ownership/control of fishing rights and processing to Icelandic citizens / Icelandic-controlled companies with **<25% foreign shareholders** *(US State Dept 2025)* — exactly the kind of restriction that was **never negotiated** and was expected to be **linked to the fisheries chapter** (below).

### The 2010–13 record (a blank on resources)
- ✅ When talks were suspended (2013), **no negotiating position was in place** for four chapters — explicitly including **free movement of capital** and **right of establishment / freedom to provide services** (alongside agriculture and fisheries). *(Iceland MFA Summary Conclusions)*
- ✅ Those two chapters were **expected to be linked to the fisheries chapter**, "especially as concerns possible limitations of foreign investment in the Icelandic fisheries sector." *(MFA Summary Conclusions)* → the ownership question for fish-as-asset was **never resolved**.
