## BOTTOM LINE

Unlike most areas of the EEA, **farming would change materially** as a full member, because agriculture sits largely *outside* the EEA. Iceland's support model today rests on **quantitative import tariffs (magntollar)** that give domestic producers "substantial protection" — virtually no imports happen outside the tariff quotas. EU membership would swap that for the **Common Agricultural Policy (CAP)** — a €387bn (2021–27) programme that supports farm *income* mainly through **decoupled, area-based direct payments** (~70% of the budget) rather than border protection.

Iceland's own accession-era analysis (Foreign Ministry, ~2009) concluded this would be a **mixed, sector-split outcome**: area-based environmental and "hard-farming"/less-favoured-area (LFA) payments would help **sheep, dairy and beef**, but would offer **essentially nothing to pork, poultry and eggs** — while the loss of tariff protection exposes all of them to free EU imports. The agriculture chapter's negotiating position was **never finished** before Iceland suspended talks in 2013 (likely internal disagreement), and the EU grants **no permanent derogations** from the common policy (only temporary ones, plus treaty-written "special solutions").

On **food security / animal health**, Iceland's prior-authorisation regime for raw meat, eggs and dairy — built around its disease-free status — has *already* been repeatedly found by the EFTA Surveillance Authority and EFTA Court to breach EEA veterinary rules. Membership would **deepen, not create**, that constraint, though a disease-risk-based safeguard could in principle survive *if* scientifically justified under WTO/SPS rules.

**The core both-sides tension:** access to substantial EU income support (and a *possible* permanent disease-based safeguard) **versus** the loss of the border protection that has underpinned Icelandic production and rural livelihoods.

## TODAY (already true) vs AS A MEMBER

### Today — under the EEA + Iceland's own tariff system
- ✅ Iceland's support rests heavily on **quantitative tariffs (magntollar)**, giving "substantial protection" — *"Svo til enginn innflutningur er stundaður utan tollkvótanna"* (virtually no imports outside the tariff quotas). *(Foreign Ministry ch.20, ~2009)*
- ✅ Agriculture is **largely outside the EEA**, so unlike most sectors this is an area where full membership genuinely changes the rules (contrast with the verðtrygging story, where the EEA already settles it).
- ✅ **Animal-health friction already exists under the EEA:** Iceland's regime for imported raw/fresh meat is *not a total ban* but a **prior-authorisation system** — importer needs a permit from the Minister of Fisheries & Agriculture plus certificates (incl. proof of **30 days' storage at −18°C** and salmonella-free status). *(ESA fact sheet)*
- ✅ The ESA and EFTA Court **repeatedly found this regime unlawful** under Council Directive **89/662/EEC** for imposing veterinary checks *beyond* the harmonised EEA checks — case **E-17/15** (*Ferskar kjötvörur ehf. v Icelandic State*); District Court of Reykjavík agreed Nov 2016; **Joined Cases E-2/17 & E-3/17** (14 Nov 2017) found a breach of Article 5 for raw/processed meat, eggs and milk. *(ESA; EFTA Court)*
- ✅ A **2015 bilateral EU–Iceland agricultural tariff agreement** (announced 17 Sept 2015; an EEA Art. 19 deal, **not** an accession outcome) phased in tariff cuts and expanded duty-free quotas over four years — Iceland gained expanded skyr/butter/lamb quotas and new poultry/pork/cheese quotas; EU duty-free access rose from ~66% to >95%; skyr quota 380→4,000 tonnes. *(Althingi doc 433; EEAS)*

### As a member — under the CAP
- ✅ Membership means **adopting the common policy, including the CAP**. Border protection isn't abolished, but it **moves to the EU's external border** — Iceland loses internal protection against the other member states (free agricultural trade within the single market). *(USDA ERS; Summary Conclusions)*
- ✅ The CAP supports farm **income** mainly via **decoupled direct payments** ("no production required," area-based), now ~70% of the budget, having replaced historic price supports. The Commission frames them as "a safety net for farmers facing lower than average incomes." *(EC ×2; USDA ERS)*
- ✅ **Basic income support (BISS)** is the single biggest tool — **~51% of the EAGF budget** (€96.69bn of €189.15bn direct payments, 2023–27) — and payments are **conditional** on environmental/land standards (GAEC), statutory management requirements, and (new for 2023–27) **social/labour** standards. *(EP factsheet 109)*

## THE LEDGER — gains, losses, uncertain

### Gains / opportunities (good-faith pro-EU side)
- ✅ **Income support for the sectors that qualify.** Area-based environmental and hard-farming/LFA payments would benefit **sheep, dairy and beef** — these would fare "betur en öðrum búgreinum" (better than other sectors). *(Foreign Ministry ch.20)*
- ✅ **Substantial EU co-financing.** Total support (EU + Icelandic) could exceed **5 billion ISK**, with the **EU share up to 65–70%**, *if* Iceland's hard-farming/LFA and eligible-production definitions resembled **northern Sweden/Finland's** (the relevant Nordic precedent). *(Foreign Ministry ch.20)*
- ✅ A **possible permanent disease-based safeguard**: Iceland *could* in principle keep restricting live-animal and raw-meat/unpasteurised-milk imports from certain EU countries on its special disease-risk status — **but only with scientific justification under WTO/SPS rules**. *(Foreign Ministry ch.20; WTO SPS Art. 5)*

### Losses / risks (good-faith anti-EU side)
- ✅ **Loss of border protection that underpins domestic production.** Free EU imports would leave the food industry "hard-pressed" and farmers facing difficulty selling their products. *(Foreign Ministry ch.20)*
- ✅ **Whole sectors left unsupported.** Pork, poultry and egg production would receive **essentially no EU funding** — they'd lose tariff protection *and* gain little CAP support. *(Foreign Ministry ch.20)*
- ✅ **No permanent derogations from the common policy.** Only temporary derogations (written into EU law, changeable only at Union level); any permanent "special solution" must be **explicitly written into the accession treaty**. *(Summary Conclusions; cf. Poland 12-yr / Croatia 7-yr land transitions; Malta's treaty-written second-home arrangement)*
- ✅ **Loss of policy control:** income drops sharply *before* CAP payments partly offset — e.g. dairy income falls by over half, beef substantially, in the ch.20 modelling. *(Foreign Ministry ch.20)*

### Honest unknowns / mixed (the dashed box)
- ✅ **Bændasamtök Íslands (Farmers' Association) is not monolithically against change.** It has argued the *existing* tariff protection "no longer has its intended effect" and that Icelandic food competitiveness keeps weakening; separately (Nov 2025) it backed adding **WTO-style safeguard measures** (GATT 1994 Art. XIX) to customs law for temporary protection against import surges. *(Note: these concern Iceland's standalone EEA trade framework, not accession directly.)* *(Althingi doc 433; umsögn 157-1038)*
- ✅ **The 2010–2013 record is a blank, not a verdict.** Iceland prepared an action plan, the EU approved it and invited a negotiating position — but the **agriculture position was never finished** when talks were suspended, "likely... a difficulty in reaching a compromise between the differing opinions within Iceland." Agriculture was one of four chapters with no position in place at suspension. *(Summary Conclusions)*

## IMPORTANT CAVEATS (carry into the chapter copy)

1. **Time-sensitivity of the sector outcomes.** The Foreign Ministry ch.20 analysis and the **5bn ISK / 65–70%** estimate date from the ~2009 accession-era report; its specific CAP figures predate the current 2023–27 CAP. They're accurate as *"what that report concluded,"* **not** as a present-day forecast. CAP structure figures (€387bn, ~70% direct payments, BISS 51%) are current and well-sourced.
2. **Don't conflate the 2015 tariff deal or Bændasamtök statements with the accession talks.** The 2015 agreement and the competitiveness/safeguard statements concern Iceland's **standalone EEA trade framework**, not the 2010–2013 EU accession negotiations.
3. **Animal-health constraints already bind Iceland as an EEA member.** Full membership would **deepen, not create**, the Directive 89/662/EEC obligations; the disease-based exemption remains **hypothetical and conditional** on SPS scientific justification.
4. **Minor source discrepancy:** USDA ERS says "~70%" direct-payments share, EC "~67%" — difference is whether market measures are bundled with Pillar I. Not material to the chapter.

## OPEN QUESTIONS / TO FINISH
1. Iceland's **net fiscal position** under the CAP — does the 5bn/65–70% estimate hold under the *current* 2023–27 CAP, and how does it compare to Iceland's present domestic agricultural budget?
2. Could Iceland realistically secure a **permanent** disease-risk SPS exemption in an accession treaty (a permanent "special solution"), or only a temporary derogation that erodes over time?
3. **Current (2026) legal status** of the raw-meat/egg/dairy import regime after the EFTA Court rulings and the 2019 salmonella special guarantees — has Iceland brought it into compliance, and what does that imply for biosecurity post-accession?
4. Concrete effects on **rural depopulation, land use, and non-farming rural employment** — sources cover farm-sector economics but not broader rural-community outcomes in depth. *(This is exactly the "rural life" half of the chapter — may warrant a targeted follow-up.)*
